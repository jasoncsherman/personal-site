# Personal Website Design Brief

## Instructions for Claude Code

Use this brief to design and build an initial working prototype of Jason Sherman's personal website.

Do not begin by building a generic portfolio template or marketing landing page. First establish a small, coherent visual system, then apply it to the homepage and representative content pages.

The result should be a usable website prototype, not a presentation explaining what the website could become.

## Project Summary

Create a personal website that functions as:

- A living archive of Jason's interests, work, learning, photographs, and personal projects
- A quiet personal billboard that helps visitors understand who Jason is
- A record of what Jason is currently exploring and becoming
- A place that can grow gradually without initially requiring a large body of work
- A credible introduction for potential collaborators without feeling sales-oriented

The site should communicate what Jason notices, values, enjoys, studies, and makes. A visitor should leave with a sense of his personality, interests, judgment, curiosity, and likely working style.

## Core Positioning

This is not a conventional professional portfolio, developer portfolio, or personal brand funnel.

It is a personal signal board for:

- What Jason is learning
- What Jason is noticing
- What Jason is making
- What Jason is thinking about
- What Jason is drawn to
- What Jason is currently working toward

The site should feel confident without pretending Jason already has an enormous archive of finished work.

## Desired Emotional Qualities

The website should feel:

- Thoughtful
- Curious
- Personal
- Calm
- Editorial
- Contemporary
- Intentionally organized
- Quietly sophisticated
- Slightly technical, but not like a developer website
- Polished, but not corporate
- Alive and actively maintained
- Comfortable containing unfinished or evolving work

It should not feel:

- Salesy
- Corporate
- Like a startup landing page
- Like a resume with decorative styling
- Like a generic creator template
- Overly technical
- Over-designed
- Artificially prolific
- Dominated by cards, gradients, or decorative effects
- Like a copy of another person's website

## Inspiration and Originality

The initial inspiration is Conor Luddy's website at:

https://www.conor.fyi/

Jason knows Conor personally and admires the site's archive-like organization, editorial restraint, metadata, compact navigation, and sense of accumulation over time.

Do not reproduce Conor's exact:

- Layout
- Color palette
- Typography pairing
- Icon sequence
- Navigation treatment
- Yellow announcement banner
- Homepage composition
- Textured background
- Photography globe
- AI-Ometer
- Metadata vocabulary
- Custom interactive flourishes

Instead, translate these abstract principles into an original system:

- A personal site can feel like a living archive
- Metadata can contribute to the visual identity
- Indexes can be more expressive than marketing sections
- Compact navigation can make the site feel like a personal tool
- Current activity should be visible
- Writing, photographs, experiments, and projects can coexist
- The design can become richer as content accumulates

The finished site should show recognizable influence at the level of philosophy, not visual imitation.

## Audience

The likely visitors include:

- Friends and acquaintances
- Potential collaborators
- People Jason meets professionally or socially
- People interested in AI learning and personal development
- People interested in Jason's photographs, writing, or projects
- Anyone trying to understand what Jason is currently doing and what he cares about

Within the first minute, a visitor should understand:

1. Who Jason is
2. What currently has his attention
3. What he cares about
4. What he is learning
5. What he is making or experimenting with
6. What catches his eye visually

## Initial Information Architecture

Build the system around these sections:

### Home

The primary personal billboard and index of the site.

It should include:

- Jason's name
- A concise personal positioning statement
- Current focus
- Selected recent writing
- Selected photo albums
- Active personal projects
- Recent AI learning entries
- Currently reading / a recent book or two
- A restrained invitation to connect or collaborate

### Writing

A home for developing a writing practice without implying Jason is already an established writer.

Possible content types:

- Notes
- Essays
- Reflections
- Field notes
- Questions
- Things Jason is figuring out

### Photos

A personal collection rather than a professional photography portfolio.

Organize photographs into albums such as:

- Places
- Walks
- Travel
- Light
- Ordinary moments
- Images Jason keeps returning to
- Seasonal or chronological collections

Albums should convey why the photographs matter, not only where and when they were taken.

### AI Log

A distinctive chronological or thematic record of Jason building his AI skill set.

Entries might cover:

- What Jason tried
- What worked
- What did not work
- Tools and workflows
- Changes in his thinking
- Useful discoveries
- Questions and concerns
- The development of taste, judgment, confidence, and agency

This section should feel human and reflective, not like technical documentation.

### Bookshelf

A living record of what Jason is reading — a natural fit for the "living archive" idea. Not a review blog; a personal shelf.

Organize books by shelf status:

- Currently reading
- Read
- To be read (TBR)
- Abandoned / set aside

Each book may carry a short note on *why it mattered* (or why he set it down) rather than a formal review. The shelf should feel intentional with only a handful of books, and grow naturally over time.

### Projects

Personal systems, experiments, and works in progress. These do not need to be technical or open source.

Possible examples:

- This personal website
- AI workflow experiments
- A personal knowledge system
- Writing practice
- Photo organization
- Learning projects
- Creative or practical systems

Projects may be active, paused, exploratory, shipped, abandoned, or revisited.

### Now

A lightweight, frequently updated snapshot of Jason's present attention.

Possible fields:

- Current focus
- Learning
- Building
- Reading
- Exploring
- Practicing
- Open to

### About / Collaborate

A concise description of:

- Jason's background
- His interests and strengths
- How he thinks and works
- The types of collaboration that energize him
- How to contact him

This page may also offer a subtle, downloadable resume/CV link for visitors who want the conventional document — kept understated and secondary, never the focus of the page.

Avoid inflated professional language and conventional sales copy.

## Visual Direction

Develop three related visual directions before committing:

1. Quiet dark archive
2. Warm editorial notebook
3. Minimal photo-forward index

The selected system may combine aspects of these, but it must remain coherent.

### Recommended baseline

- Editorial page composition
- Restrained interface chrome
- Strong typography
- Generous but controlled spacing
- One primary reading column
- Optional contextual side rails on large screens
- Fine borders and metadata rules
- Limited use of framed surfaces
- A single distinctive accent color
- Dark-first or genuinely balanced dark/light themes

Avoid making every section a floating card.

### Typography

Use a maximum of three typographic roles:

- Display/editorial type for page titles
- Highly readable body type for prose
- Monospaced or compact type for metadata

Typography should feel personal and literate, not futuristic or aggressively technical.

Do not copy the exact fonts used by the inspiration site.

### Color

Create an original palette with:

- Primary background
- Secondary surface
- Primary text
- Secondary text
- Muted text
- Border
- Accent
- Soft accent
- Success/error colors for functional states

The palette should not depend on gradients.

### Motion

Motion should be brief and purposeful:

- Subtle page transitions
- Clear hover and focus feedback
- Gentle reveal of changing content
- Optional reading progress or timeline movement

Support `prefers-reduced-motion`.

Avoid ambient movement that distracts from reading or photographs.

## Navigation

Explore a compact navigation system inspired by the idea of a personal toolbelt or index.

Possible approaches:

- Compact icon-and-label navigation
- Short text navigation
- Hybrid navigation with icons and revealed labels

Requirements:

- Navigation must remain understandable without hovering
- Icon-only controls must have accessible labels
- Touch targets must be at least approximately 40-44px
- The current section must be clearly indicated visually and programmatically
- Mobile navigation must not feel crowded

Do not reproduce the inspiration site's exact icon dock.

## Responsive Behavior

Design desktop and mobile together.

Desktop may use:

- A central reading column
- A contextual left rail
- A secondary right rail

Mobile should:

- Collapse side-rail information into inline sections or compact controls
- Keep typography readable
- Preserve generous spacing
- Avoid horizontal scrolling
- Keep navigation and controls comfortably tappable
- Avoid placing persistent banners over content

## Accessibility

The prototype should include:

- Semantic HTML
- Logical heading structure
- Visible keyboard focus
- Skip navigation
- Accessible names for controls
- Sufficient color contrast
- Proper form labels
- Useful photo alt text
- Reduced-motion support
- Keyboard-operable filters and navigation

Accessibility should be part of the visual system, not a later patch.

## Content Strategy for a Small Initial Archive

The website must feel intentional with limited content.

Assume an initial launch could contain:

- 1 homepage
- 1 Now page
- 1 About/Collaborate page
- 2 short writing entries
- 2 photo albums
- 2 AI log entries
- 2 or 3 books on the shelf
- 1 or 2 personal projects

Do not fill the prototype with dozens of fake entries. Use a small amount of clearly marked placeholder content where Jason has not supplied final copy.

Do not fabricate professional achievements, experience, opinions, locations, or personal history.

## Content Management

Prefer a system Jason can maintain without being a developer.

Recommended first approach:

- Local Markdown or MDX content
- Clearly named folders
- Simple front matter
- Image folders associated with albums
- No database required for the first version

The content model should make adding a note, project, AI log, or photo album straightforward.

Document the editing workflow in plain language.

## Technical Direction

Choose a low-maintenance stack suitable for a content-first personal site.

Preferred characteristics:

- Static generation
- Excellent image handling
- Markdown/MDX support
- Easy deployment
- Good performance
- Minimal ongoing maintenance
- Strong accessibility defaults

**Decision (2026-06-15): Astro.** Chosen over Jason's usual Next.js because this is a content-first static site where Astro is the right-sized tool — simpler, faster, Markdown-native, minimal ongoing maintenance. The learning cost is low (the fundamentals transfer), and choosing the right tool for the job is a stronger portfolio signal alongside his existing Next.js apps. Build: Astro + TypeScript + @astrojs/mdx + content collections (Zod-typed front matter), deployed on Vercel.

Do not introduce a CMS, authentication, database, analytics, comments, or newsletter integration in the initial prototype unless specifically requested.

## Acceptance Criteria

The prototype is successful when:

- It feels personal rather than templated
- It communicates Jason's interests and current direction quickly
- It looks intentional with very little content
- It does not resemble a developer portfolio
- It does not closely imitate Conor's site
- Writing, photos, AI learning, and projects feel like parts of one system
- Visitors can understand how Jason thinks and what he cares about
- The site provides a credible path toward contact or collaboration
- Jason can add content without understanding the entire codebase
- Desktop and mobile layouts both feel considered
- The site is accessible and fast
